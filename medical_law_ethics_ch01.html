Why Study Law and Ethics?
Professional excellence
To help you function at the highest possible professional level, providing competent, ethical, lawful healthcare to patients. Knowledge of law and ethics is not optional — it is a core professional competency.
Legal self-protection
To help you avoid legal entanglements that can threaten your ability to earn a living. A single lawsuit or license revocation can end a healthcare career — even if the practitioner was not at fault.
Litigious society
We live in a litigious society — patients, relatives, and others are inclined to sue healthcare practitioners, facilities, equipment manufacturers, and others when medical outcomes are unacceptable. Healthcare professionals must understand their legal exposure.
Focus Areas of Law & Ethics in Healthcare
Rights, responsibilities & concerns of consumers
What healthcare consumers (patients) are legally entitled to — including informed consent, privacy, non-discrimination, and quality of care. Their responsibilities as partners in care.
Legal and ethical issues facing society
From end-of-life decisions to genetic privacy to reproductive rights — healthcare intersects with some of society's most contested ethical and legal debates. Practitioners must navigate these thoughtfully.
Impact of rising costs
Cost constraints shape healthcare delivery decisions in ways that create legal and ethical tensions — rationing, insurance denials, resource allocation, and access disparities all have law and ethics dimensions.

Critical distinction: Law sets the floor (minimum required conduct). Ethics builds above the floor toward ideal conduct. Moral values are the personal foundation that ethics is built on. Bioethics applies ethical reasoning to medicine and biological research. Etiquette and protocol govern professional courtesy and workplace manners respectively.
Law — Detailed
Definition: A rule of conduct or action formally recognized as binding by a controlling authority (government). Law is not a suggestion — it is compulsory and enforced through the power of the state.

Purpose: Establishes the minimum standard necessary to keep society functioning. Law does not demand ethical excellence — only baseline compliance.

Enforcement: Made possible by penalties for disobedience — fines, imprisonment, or both. In healthcare, penalties also include revocation of professional license, which can permanently end a career.
Key insight: A healthcare professional can act legally but unethically — or ethically but run afoul of a specific regulation. Law and ethics overlap but are not identical. Understanding both is essential.
Ethics — Detailed
Definition: Standards of behavior and the concept of right and wrong — going beyond legal minimum toward ideals of good conduct.

Foundation: Moral values formed through the influence of family, culture, and society serve as the basis for ethical conduct. Ethics builds on these personal values, then applies them to professional standards.

Bioethics: A specialized discipline dealing with the ethical implications of biological research and medicine. Bioethicists (medical ethicists) consult with medical professionals to help navigate difficult decisions — end-of-life care, experimental treatment, genetic engineering, organ allocation.
Ethics committees: Found primarily in hospital settings. Members include physicians, nurses, social workers, clergy, family members, community representatives, and others. Purpose: determine possible solutions to difficult, multi-dimensional healthcare ethical issues.
Litigation Terminology — Key Definitions
Plaintiff
The person bringing charges in a lawsuit; the party who files the legal action claiming to have been harmed by the defendant. In medical malpractice cases, usually the patient (or their estate).
Defendant
The person against whom charges are brought; the party sued. In medical malpractice cases, usually the healthcare provider, facility, or product manufacturer.
Liable
Legally responsible or obligated. A liable party is found by a court to be responsible for harm and may be required to pay damages or face other penalties.
Precedent
Decisions made by judges in various courts that become the rule of law and apply to future cases with similar facts. Also called case law or common law. Once established, lower courts within the same jurisdiction must follow the precedent (stare decisis — "let the decision stand").
Summary judgment
A decision made by a court that there is no genuine dispute of material fact and the case does not need to go to trial. One party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. Granted when the evidence is so one-sided that no reasonable jury could decide differently.
Product Liability in Healthcare
Manufacturers of healthcare equipment and product dealers can be held legally responsible for defective medical devices and products. This is called product liability — an important area of healthcare law distinct from medical malpractice.
Breach of warranty
The product does not perform as promised. An expressed warranty (written/verbal promise) or implied warranty (expectation of basic safety and fitness for use) has been violated. Example: a surgical implant marketed as permanent fails prematurely.
Untrue statements
Misrepresentations made about the product's safety, effectiveness, or characteristics — whether in advertising, labeling, or direct communication to healthcare providers or patients.
Defective products
Products with a manufacturing defect (something went wrong in production), design defect (the entire design is inherently unsafe), or a failure to warn (inadequate instructions or warnings about known risks).
Fraud
Intentional deceit — knowingly misrepresenting a product to induce purchase or use. Fraud is both a civil and criminal matter. In healthcare, fraud carries particularly severe penalties including exclusion from federal healthcare programs.
Medtronic case — federal preemption of medical devices
Makers of medical equipment are generally immune from personal injury liability if the FDA approved the product and it meets FDA specifications. The extent of this immunity has evolved — a 2008 U.S. Supreme Court ruling in Medtronic, Inc. v. Riegel held that FDA pre-market approval preempts most state tort claims for Class III medical devices. This creates a significant shield for device manufacturers.
Wyeth v. Levine (2009) — drug label liability
Federal preemption doctrine can bar injured consumers from suing in state court when products met federal standards. However, Wyeth v. Levine (2009) became an important precedent for future drug labeling cases — the Supreme Court held that federal drug labeling requirements do NOT automatically preempt state failure-to-warn claims, meaning pharmaceutical companies can face state lawsuits even if their label met FDA requirements. This creates important contrast with the Medtronic ruling for device makers.
Early Medical Codes of Ethics
Code of Hammurabi (~1754 BCE)
One of the oldest written legal codes; included specific provisions governing physicians, including standards of care and specific penalties for medical errors. Established the principle that practitioners are accountable for their work.
Hippocratic Oath (~400 BCE)
A pledge for physicians influenced by the practices of the Greek physician Hippocrates. Established foundational principles still reflected in modern medical ethics: benefit patients, do no harm, maintain confidentiality, refer when beyond competence, and treat all patients honorably. The phrase "First, do no harm" (primum non nocere) derives from Hippocratic tradition.
Percival's Medical Ethics (1803)
Thomas Percival's Medical Ethics was the first systematic modern code of medical ethics. It influenced the American Medical Association's first code of ethics in 1847 and established the template for professional medical codes that followed.
Historical significance: These three documents show that the need to regulate medical practice ethically has existed for nearly 4,000 years. The Hippocratic tradition remains the moral foundation of contemporary medical ethics codes.
Contemporary Codes of Ethics
Modern professional codes of ethics share four common characteristics.
Established by the appropriate professional organization
Each healthcare profession has its own organization that creates and maintains the code specific to that discipline (AMA for physicians, AAMA for medical assistants, etc.)
Govern the behavior of members
The code sets behavioral expectations for all members of that professional organization; members agree to be bound by it upon joining
Increase competence and standards of care
By defining ethical conduct, codes raise the overall standard of practice within the profession and protect patients from substandard care
Do NOT have legal authority
Codes of ethics are professional standards — NOT laws. Violation results in professional consequences (expulsion, censure) but NOT criminal prosecution or civil liability directly from the code itself
Healthcare organizations with codes of ethics:
AAMA (Medical Assistants) AHIMA (Health Info Management) AHA (Hospitals) AMA (Physicians) ASRT (Radiologic Technologists)
AAMA Code of Ethics for Medical Assistants — Full Text
The Code of Ethics for Medical Assistants (American Association of Medical Assistants) contains five principles that define ethical professional conduct. These are commonly tested.
1
Render service with full respect for the dignity of humanity — treat every patient as an individual deserving of compassion, respect, and quality care regardless of race, religion, national origin, sex, disability, or any other characteristic
2
Respect confidential information obtained through employment — maintain patient privacy and confidentiality; do not disclose information learned in the course of patient care without authorization; HIPAA compliance is the legal counterpart to this ethical obligation
3
Uphold the honor and high principles of the profession and accept its disciplines — represent the profession positively; follow professional standards; accept the consequences of ethical violations if they occur
4
Seek to continually improve the knowledge and skills of medical assistants — pursue ongoing professional development; maintain competency through continuing education; stay current with evolving standards and technologies
5
Participate in additional service activities aimed at improving the health of the community — extend professional service beyond the workplace into the broader community; contribute to public health and health education
Source: American Association of Medical Assistants (AAMA) — www.aama-ntl.org. Note that the code establishes positive obligations ("render," "respect," "uphold," "seek," "participate") — it describes what medical assistants MUST do, not just what they must avoid.
Hippocratic Oath — Key Principles
The original Hippocratic Oath (ancient Greek text) established several ethical principles that remain foundational in modern medical ethics. Key commitments include:
Benefit the patient
"I will follow that method of treatment which, according to my ability and judgment, I consider for the benefit of my patients." Patient welfare is the primary obligation.
Do no harm
"Abstain from whatever is deleterious and mischievous." The principle of non-maleficence — avoid actions that harm patients. Basis of the principle primum non nocere ("First, do no harm").
Refuse to assist with harmful acts
"I will give no deadly medicine to anyone if asked." Refusal to participate in euthanasia. Historical basis for modern debates about physician-assisted suicide.
Maintain confidentiality
"Whatever I may see or hear in the lives of men which ought not to be spoken abroad, I will not divulge." The earliest articulation of patient confidentiality — precursor to HIPAA.
Enter only for patient benefit
"Into whatever houses I enter I will go into them for the benefit of the sick." The physician's sole purpose in the clinical relationship is patient welfare.
Act with purity and holiness
"With purity and holiness, I will pass my life and practice my art." The practitioner's entire life — not just clinical hours — should reflect professional values.
Qualities for Success in Healthcare Careers
🙏
Courtesy
The practice of good manners. In healthcare, courtesy creates a therapeutic environment that reduces patient anxiety and builds trust. Greet patients by name, maintain eye contact, knock before entering a room, and acknowledge patients who are waiting.
❤️
Compassion
Empathy — the ability to understand and share the feelings of another. Compassion motivates practitioners to go beyond technical competence to address patients' emotional and psychological needs. Patients remember how you made them feel.
💡
Common sense
Sound practical judgment. The ability to recognize what is appropriate in a given situation without needing a rule for every scenario. Knowing when to ask for help, how to prioritize tasks, and how to respond calmly in unexpected situations.
🤝
People skills
Traits and capabilities that allow you to get along well with others — colleagues, patients, families, and supervisors. Includes communication, active listening, conflict resolution, teamwork, and cultural sensitivity. Often called "soft skills" but arguably the most important skills in healthcare.
🔬
Technical skills
Abilities acquired in the course of study — clinical procedures, equipment operation, documentation, pharmacology, anatomy, and all the specialized knowledge and manual skills required for competent practice. Technical competence is the foundation of patient safety.
🧠
Critical thinking skills
Problem-solving skills — the ability to analyze information, evaluate evidence, consider alternatives, and make sound decisions. In healthcare, critical thinking is essential for recognizing deteriorating patients, identifying errors, and choosing the right course of action under pressure.
Integration: These six qualities work together. Technical skills without compassion produces technically correct but cold care. Compassion without technical skills is well-intentioned but unsafe. Critical thinking without people skills produces the right solution delivered badly. Excellence requires all six.
The Critical Thinking / Problem-Solving Process
Critical thinking is a systematic, disciplined process of evaluating information and reasoning to reach a well-justified conclusion. In healthcare, applying this process protects patients and practitioners.
1
Identify and clarify the problem
Define what the actual issue is — not the symptom, but the root cause. Ask: "What specifically is wrong?" and "What outcome do we need?" In healthcare: Is this an ethical dilemma? A legal question? A clinical problem? Clarity at step 1 prevents wasted effort.
2
Gather information
Collect all relevant facts, data, policies, laws, and perspectives. In healthcare: What does the chart say? What is the patient's stated preference? What does the policy require? What does the law say? Do not rush to judgment before gathering complete information.
3
Evaluate the evidence
Assess the quality and reliability of information gathered. Consider: Is this source credible? Is this information current? Does it apply to this specific situation? Distinguish facts from opinions, and evidence from assumptions.
4
Consider alternatives and implications
Generate possible courses of action and think through the consequences of each. Who will be affected? What are the legal implications? What are the ethical implications? What are the practical constraints? Play out each scenario before committing.
5
Choose and implement the best alternative
Select the option that best serves the patient, complies with law and ethics, and is practically achievable. Then ACT — implement decisively, document appropriately, and monitor results. Be prepared to adjust if outcomes are not as expected.
Determining If a Decision Is Ethical — 5 Self-Check Questions
Before taking an action in a gray area, ask yourself these five questions. If you cannot answer "yes" (or feel comfortable) with all of them, reconsider your choice.
1
Will you have followed both the law AND your organization's policies? — Ethical action starts with legal and policy compliance. If your planned action violates either, it is not ethical regardless of your intentions. This is the baseline test.
2
Will you promote a win-win situation as best as possible? — Ethical decisions in healthcare strive for outcomes that benefit all parties — patient, provider, and organization. If your decision clearly benefits you at the patient's expense (or vice versa), that's a red flag.
3
How would you react if this act were publicized in the newspaper or other media? — The "newspaper test." If you would be embarrassed, defensive, or alarmed to see your action reported publicly, reconsider. Ethical actions hold up to public scrutiny.
4
Would you want your family members to know? — A more personal version of the newspaper test. If you would be ashamed for your family to know what you did, that shame is a moral signal worth heeding. Professional pride and personal integrity should align.
5
Can you look at yourself in a mirror? — The integrity test. Can you honestly tell yourself that you did the right thing? Your own conscience — informed by your moral values and professional ethics — is a critical check. If you have to rationalize extensively, the action is probably questionable.
Practical application: Use these five questions whenever you face an ethically ambiguous situation — a patient request that feels uncomfortable, a supervisor instruction that seems wrong, a shortcut that seems harmless. If any question gives you pause, investigate further before acting.
